Coordinate geometry is nothing but the graphical representation of math. It involves plotting data so that it can studied and analyzed easily. It basically consists of two axes - X and Y, where X extends to the right direction and Y extends to the upward direction. The begin from a point which we call the origin, which is basically a point from which distances are measured(all this will make sense in a minute, hang on). Below is how this looks like.

As you can see in the above picture, there are two lines, one is horizontal and another is vertical. As mentioned earlier, X axis is the horizontal axis and Y axis is the vertical axis. There is a point marked O, which is known as the "origin". It is the point of intersection of the axes(plural of axis), and is the point from which we measure distances on this plane.

By distances, what I mean is that we can measure the seperation between two things using this. As you can see in the above picture, we have points marked at unit distances from each other. As in the x axis, we have on the right side the point 1 marked 1 unit to the right of the origin, and 2 marked 2 unit to the right of origin and 1 unit to the right of point 1. Similar case happens in the upward direction for the Y axis.

But what is this deal with the negative numbers on the plane? These numbers follow the same concept of unit distances from each other, these are just used to represent the directions opposite to the conventional axis direction. For instance, the negative numbers of the left of the X axis(see above) can be seen to be used to represent distances in a direction opposite to the conventional X axis direction(right), which is left. Similarly, negative numbers on the y axis point in the downward direction instead of up. This is basically the "negative" direction of the axis, and we will get to know their utility soon too.

Slope of a line

Have you ever been to a hilly area for a vacation, or is it that you live in a hilly area. If yes, then you might have often heard of the word slope. Someone might have said that: "the hill has high slope, we should not be climbing this". But what is this slope, and why are we even learning geography in physics???

Relax, it is not geography. You should be asking, why are we including math in geography? Yes, this term is related to and originates from mathematics.

Slope is the inclination of a line. It represents how tilted a line is. Let me elaborate.

Let us take the previous line y = 2x + 3 which we plotted earlier.

We take any two points on this line. Let us say we take any random two from the table we had, say we take (1, 5) and (2, 7). Now the slope of this line is essentially the ratio of the differnces of y coordinates and x coordinates. Mathematically:

$$\text{Slope} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

where y2 and y1 are the y coordinates of the two points chosen, and x2 and x1 are the x coordinates of the two points chosen.
